Maps

As part of lab, here is my map collection.

2015 USA Retail Opportunity

This is a map representing the 2015 United States Retail Opportunities with an adjustable zoom to decipher opportunity from the state scale, county scale, zip code, census tract and down to block group. The map defines market opportunity by measuring total retail businesses in an area, annual retail sales and annual retail sales potential in that area.

2014-2019 USA Population Growth Rate

The above is the anticipated growth rate by block group, census tract, zip code, county or state dependent on zoom. This can be a good indicator of where more or less resources will be needed in the future.

World Growth Indicator (by Country)

A Google Fusion Table showing countries adjusted net national income (annual % growth). World Bank staff estimates based on sources and methods in World Bank's "The Changing Wealth of Nations: Measuring Sustainable Development in the New Millennium".

1. My maps display different economic factors. I selected the data because it could be useful for my capstone project that will incorporate data as it relates to retail, consumption and economic growth. The data is from Esri's ArcGIS Online and the World Bank.

2. I used Esri AGO for my first map. I have used AGO before but have never practiced embedding my web map in a website (let alone MY website!). The second mapping platform I chose was Google Fusion Tables. I selected Google Fusion Tables because it is a Google product I had not heard of. The two platforms were VERY different for producing maps. The biggest difference to me was focusing on producing a table in Google Fusion before I could advance to the mapping interface.

3. Esri AGO is a relatively easy platform to use with a fairly inherent interface. They also have a lot of data that is already available online and provides easy interactive map production. I had an extremely difficult time using Google Fusion Tables. Perhaps it was because I was new to the process, but I struggled with geocoding the data I downloaded. I downloaded international data and had difficulties getting it organized due to navigating all of the metadata as well as deleting and inserting rows/columns. Google Fusion also has a few bugs, for example, it would not let me delete rows in my table, causing unwanted data to remain in my map. Overall, Esri's AGO is MUCH easier for me to use.

4. I think I can produce much better maps with ArcGIS Online. This shouldn't be a surprise since Esri is in the business of mapmaking. AGO clearly provides the mapmaker with a vast assortment of options in relation to map elements such as panning, thematic representation, color, search bar, legend production and they even help organize the map layout. The process is rather deliberate in the sense that the mapmaker feels some constraint, however I am sure when our skills advance into open source mapmaking that we will be comfortable in our abilities to create a map exactly how we imagine it with Esri's API. Google Fusion on the other hand hardly has any editing options such as making a map transparent, nor do they have large options for visual variables such as color schemes or icons. Google Fusion also only has two basemap options (map and satellite) whereas AGO lets you choose from many. Google Fusion seems to be for programmers because it requires more custom code to personalize maps. I think the end-user would have a fairly easy time navigating and understanding either of these maps. They are relatively basic and provide an intuitive means of panning and clicking on location to explore data.

5. I did not attempt any analysis on these maps. However, as I mentioned I did struggle with organizing my data as well as successfully geocoding it in order for it to function in Google Fusion. I also had difficulties with adding some things to my website. I found some cool CSS tricks that I could not get to appear on my web page, likely cause I was placing it in the wrong part of either the CSS or HTML. I'll keep working at it!
